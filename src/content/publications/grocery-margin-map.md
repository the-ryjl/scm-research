## Why a margin map?

Low-margin retail businesses can experience meaningful earnings changes from small movements in gross margin, labor productivity, or shrink. A margin map keeps those drivers separate and makes it harder to attribute every change to pricing.

## Illustrative bridge

| Driver | Margin effect |
| --- | ---: |
| Price and mix | +35 bps |
| Vendor funding | +10 bps |
| Shrink | -20 bps |
| Labor | -15 bps |
| Occupancy and other | -5 bps |

The values above are invented. In real work, every line would be supported with filings, management commentary, and clearly labeled estimates.

## Store-level questions

What is happening to units per basket? Are promotions driving traffic or only shifting timing? Is labor productivity improving because of process design or because service levels are falling? Does inventory turn improvement reflect better replenishment or out-of-stocks?

## Educational conclusion

The map is useful because it converts a single reported margin into a set of operating hypotheses. Those hypotheses can then be tested through filings, store observations, and ethical primary research.
