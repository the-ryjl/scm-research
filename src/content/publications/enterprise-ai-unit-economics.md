## Start with the customer job

The most useful first question is not which model is largest. It is what job the customer is paying to complete. A workflow that reduces review time, improves conversion, or lowers error rates can support very different economics from a novelty feature with weak retention.

## The cost stack

An enterprise AI product may combine model access, inference compute, data preparation, security, human review, and implementation support. A simple gross-margin estimate can miss costs that live below the reported cost-of-revenue line.

| Cost layer | Illustrative share of revenue | Key sensitivity |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Model and compute | 18% | Usage intensity |
| Implementation | 12% | Customer complexity |
| Support and review | 9% | Error tolerance |
| Other delivery cost | 6% | Scale and mix |

## Questions for primary research

1. Which workflows remain in production after the pilot period?
2. Who owns the implementation budget?
3. Does usage expand after deployment, and does value expand at the same rate?
4. Can the vendor reprice as model and infrastructure costs change?

## Takeaway

The strongest educational framework connects technical capability to a measurable customer outcome, then traces how the resulting value is divided among infrastructure, model, application, and service providers.
