## Research question

What evidence would distinguish a durable consumer brand from a product experiencing a temporary burst of attention? The question matters because surface-level growth can come from discounting, distribution expansion, or unusually favorable category conditions. None of those alone establishes pricing power.

Our placeholder company, **Northstar Goods**, is not a real issuer. We use it to demonstrate a research workflow that begins with customer behavior, connects that behavior to unit economics, and ends with explicit disconfirming evidence.

## A three-part durability test

### 1. Repeat behavior before reach

Distribution growth can make almost any young brand look healthy. A stronger signal is whether customers return without a larger promotional push. A student analyst could track cohorts, survey buyers, compare reorder intervals, and study changes in full-price sell-through.

### 2. Price realization, not list price

List prices often rise while realized prices stagnate. The gap can hide in couponing, channel mix, or product bundling. A useful bridge starts with reported revenue and separates volume, mix, direct price, and promotions.

These values are invented for demonstration and should not be treated as market data.

### 3. Distribution that compounds

More doors are not always better. High-quality distribution improves discovery while preserving economics and brand positioning. Low-quality expansion can create inventory pressure and train customers to wait for markdowns.

## Scenario framework

Rather than publishing one point estimate, we would separate the thesis into three paths:

- **Base case:** moderate category growth, stable repeat behavior, and no further promotional increase.
- **Upside case:** better retention allows marketing efficiency to improve as the customer base grows.
- **Downside case:** wholesale expansion pulls demand forward and compresses realized pricing.

## What would change our mind

The thesis would weaken if repeat purchase fell across mature cohorts, if inventory growth persistently exceeded sales growth, or if customer acquisition costs rose without stronger contribution margins. Stating those conditions before a simulated investment helps reduce narrative drift.

## Conclusion

The goal of this framework is not to label every growing brand a winner or a fad. It is to make the burden of proof explicit. A resilient brand should show customer pull, measurable price realization, and distribution economics that improve—not deteriorate—as it scales.
